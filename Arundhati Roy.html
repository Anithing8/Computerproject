Arundhati Roy

Arundhati Roy is an Indian author, activist, and former actress. She is best known for her novel The God of Small Things, which won the Booker Prize in 1997. She is also a vocal critic of globalization, neoliberalism, and militarism in India.

Early life and career

Roy was born in Shillong, Meghalaya, to a Bengali father and a Syrian Christian mother. Her parents divorced when she was two, and she moved to Kerala with her mother and brother. She studied architecture at the School of Planning and Architecture in Delhi, where she met her first husband, Gerard da Cunha. She later married filmmaker Pradip Krishen, who gave her a role in his movie Massey Sahib in 1985. She also wrote the screenplays for In Which Annie Gives It Those Ones (1989) and Electric Moon (1992).

Literary works

Roy’s debut novel The God of Small Things was published in 1997 and became an international bestseller. It tells the story of a family in Kerala and the tragic consequences of their forbidden love. The novel explores themes such as caste, class, gender, religion, and politics in India. It won the Booker Prize in 1997 and was translated into more than 40 languages.

After the success of her novel, Roy became more involved in social and political activism. She wrote several essays on topics such as nuclear weapons, dams, Kashmir, Maoism, and the US invasion of Iraq. Some of her essay collections are The Cost of Living (1999), Power Politics (2001), War Talk (2003), An Ordinary Person’s Guide to Empire (2004), and The End of Imagination (2016).

Roy returned to fiction after a gap of 20 years with her second novel The Ministry of Utmost Happiness, which was published in 2017. The novel spans several decades and follows the lives of various characters across India, from Delhi to Kashmir to Kerala. It deals with issues such as communal violence, corruption, environmental degradation, and human rights violations. The novel was longlisted for the Booker Prize in 2017 and was shortlisted for the National Book Critics Circle Award in 2018.

Awards and honors

Roy has received many awards and honors for her literary and activist work. Some of them are:

  • The Booker Prize (1997) for The God of Small Things
  • The Lannan Cultural Freedom Prize (2002) for her "courageous and visionary" work as an activist
  • The Sydney Peace Prize (2004) for her "commitment to global justice"
  • The Norman Mailer Prize (2011) for Distinguished Writing
  • The Ambedkar Sudar Award (2015) for her contribution to Dalit literature
  • The Mahmoud Darwish Award (2019) for her "creativity, courage, and vision"
