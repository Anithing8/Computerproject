Munshi Premchand

Munshi Premchand was one of the greatest Indian writers of Hindi and Urdu literature in the early 20th century. He was a novelist, short story writer, dramatist, and translator who wrote over a dozen novels, hundreds of short stories, and numerous essays. He also used the pen names Nawab Rai and Dhanpat Rai Srivastav. He was known as the "Kalam Ka Sipahi" (Soldier of the Pen) and the "Upanyas Samrat" (Emperor of Novels)

Premchand was born on 31 July 1880 in Lamhi, a village near Varanasi, where his father was a clerk in the post office. His parents died young - his mother when he was seven and his father while he was fourteen and still a student. He had to face immense poverty and hardship in his life. He married twice and had four children. He worked as a teacher, a deputy sub-inspector of schools, and an editor of various magazines. He also participated in the Indian independence movement and quit his government job as a part of the non-cooperation movement. He died on 8 October 1936 in Varanasi at the age of 56

Some of his famous works are Godaan (The Gift of a Cow), Nirmala (The Pure One), Karmabhoomi (The Land of Action), Gaban (Embezzlement), Sevasadan (House of Service), Shatranj Ke Khiladi (The Chess Players), Idgah (The Eid Fair), Poos Ki Raat (The Night of Winter), Bade Ghar Ki Beti (The Daughter of a Rich House), and Kafan (The Shroud). His works have been translated into many languages and have inspired generations of readers and writers. He received many awards and honors for his literary contributions, such as the Sahitya Akademi Award, the Padma Shri, and the Bharat Ratna (posthumously).
